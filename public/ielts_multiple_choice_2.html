Coral Reefs in Danger

Coral reefs are among the most biologically diverse ecosystems on Earth, supporting roughly 25 percent of all known marine species despite covering less than one percent of the ocean floor. They are formed over thousands of years by tiny organisms called coral polyps, which secrete a hard limestone skeleton. Over time, these skeletons accumulate to form the massive structures we recognise as reefs.

However, coral reefs around the world are under severe threat. Rising ocean temperatures caused by climate change lead to a phenomenon known as coral bleaching. When water becomes too warm, corals expel the colourful algae living inside their tissues, turning white. Without these algae, which provide up to 90 percent of a coral's energy through photosynthesis, the coral slowly starves and eventually dies if temperatures do not return to normal.

Ocean acidification presents another major danger. As the ocean absorbs increasing amounts of carbon dioxide from the atmosphere, seawater becomes more acidic. This makes it harder for coral polyps to build their calcium carbonate skeletons, weakening existing reef structures and slowing the growth of new ones. Scientists estimate that if current trends continue, more than 70 percent of the world's coral reefs could disappear by 2050.
