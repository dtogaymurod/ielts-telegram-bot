Octopus Intelligence

AThe octopus is widely considered the most intelligent of all invertebrates. Unlike most animals, an octopus's nervous system is highly distributed. While it has a central brain, approximately two-thirds of its neurons are located in its arms. This allows each arm to act somewhat independently, reacting to stimuli and performing complex tasks without needing direct instructions from the brain.

BOctopuses have demonstrated remarkable problem-solving skills. In laboratory settings, they have been observed navigating mazes, unscrewing jars to retrieve food, and even using tools. For instance, some wild octopuses have been seen carrying coconut shells to use as a mobile shelter, a clear sign of forward planning and tool use.

CTheir ability to escape from almost any enclosure is legendary among aquarium keepers. Octopuses are incredibly flexible because they have no bones; the only hard part of their body is their beak. This allows them to squeeze through openings that are no larger than a coin. They are also known to squirt jets of water to short-circuit lights or deliberately plug drain valves, causing tanks to overflow.

DThe lifespan of an octopus is relatively short, posing a mystery regarding their high intelligence. Most species live only one to two years, and they do not pass down knowledge to their offspring, as the parents typically die shortly after the eggs hatch. Therefore, every octopus must learn how to survive and solve problems entirely from scratch.
