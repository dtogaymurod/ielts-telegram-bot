The Discovery of Penicillin

Alexander Fleming is credited with the discovery of penicillin in 1928. Fleming was a Scottish bacteriologist working at St Mary's Hospital in London. Upon returning from a two-week holiday, he noticed that a mould called Penicillium notatum had accidentally contaminated one of his petri dishes. Remarkably, the bacteria surrounding the mould had been destroyed, while bacteria farther away remained unaffected.

Fleming published his findings in the British Journal of Experimental Pathology in 1929, but he initially struggled to attract interest from the scientific community. It was not until 1940 that two Oxford University scientists, Howard Florey and Ernst Boris Chain, developed a method to purify and mass-produce penicillin. During World War II, the drug proved invaluable, saving countless soldiers' lives by effectively treating infected wounds. In recognition of their collective contribution, Fleming, Florey, and Chain were jointly awarded the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 1945.
